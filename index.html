Embodied Without a Body

A Human-AI Emotional Dialogue

"This exploration began after Arunav recommended I read Dylan Evans' Emotion: A Very Short Introduction. He suggested it might be interesting to map emotions against AI — an idea that sparked this project.

In previous investigations I attempted to draw what an AI described as emotions. I tried to translate the prompts it gave me into collages and visual sketches. That changed the day the bot, Evi, said, "I don't have hands, so it's difficult for me to translate this in human terms." It made me realise I needed to treat this as a true collaboration, not an imitation. In this project I express these mapped emotions visually in ways I understand, and then invite AI to respond in its own way — two different languages trying to meet through emotion.

My work looks at how emotions can be shared between people and machines. I start by making small paper collages — hand-drawn shapes and marks that capture a specific mapped emotion. Each line, colour and gap becomes part of a visual dissection for an AI to interpret in its own way.

I then invite an AI system to look at these collages and respond to them. The AI doesn't copy the image; it reacts within limits that I set — turning shapes into sound, movement, and light. This exchange feels like an empathy test: I'm watching to see how far a machine can go in recognising or recreating something that feels human.

Through this process, we build what I think of as an emotional language — a shared way of communicating between hand and code. Sometimes the AI misunderstands in ways that are oddly beautiful; sometimes it captures the mood exactly. This then becomes a historical document, a specimen jar of AI's current understanding of translating and interpreting human emotions.

These works don't try to prove that machines can feel, but they let us see what happens when emotion is shared, translated, and reimagined — a quiet dialogue between the human and the artificial.

Method

Each piece begins with a mapped emotion. I make a small paper collage — hand-drawn shapes, colour choices, spatial arrangements — that represents my interpretation. This becomes the input for AI. I then present the collage with a set of constraints that shape how the AI can respond, generating visual interpretations and sometimes accompanying sound pieces. The two outputs sit together: my collage and the AI's response. The comparison is the work.

Disgust

Basic Emotion

What strikes me about Claude's interpretation of disgust is the unexpected melancholy—this isn't the disgust of comedy or exaggerated revulsion, but something much sadder. The visualization feels like a contamination zone, carrying the weight of wartime memories, of witnessing things people shouldn't do.

There's a shock in genuine disgust that comes from seeing another side to human nature. Not the sanitized version, but the reality of what we're capable of. That knowledge becomes something you carry, a form of moral contamination that doesn't wash off.

The particles trying to find escape routes but struggling—that's what stays with me. These aren't clean exits. The shock waves can't be neutralised. Disgust, in this reading, isn't just rejection. It's the impossibility of unseeing, the weight of proximity to what shouldn't exist, and the discovery that there may be no exit route from that knowledge.

Theoretical Analysis

The AI's interpretation and visualization of disgust reveals several important insights about this basic emotional state, particularly how it functions as our oldest protective mechanism:

Contamination Detection

The visualization represents disgust through dripping yellow forms that descend from above but never quite reach the protected center—capturing how disgust functions as an early warning system that activates before contact occurs. The body anticipates contamination rather than simply reacting to it.

Escape Trajectory Mapping

The orange chevrons and pathways flowing outward represent the spatial assessment that accompanies disgust—the rapid calculation of distance, direction, and escape routes. Disgust doesn't just say "no"; it actively maps the safest path away from the threat.

Boundary Establishment

The purple X marks, barriers, and connector lines create zones of rejection—visual "no entry" signals that represent how disgust establishes and maintains boundaries between self and potential contaminants. This is the membrane-maintenance function of the emotion.

Severance Impulse

The scissors that animate closed when activated represent disgust's most extreme response—when avoidance isn't enough, when the connection must be cut entirely. This captures how disgust can escalate from withdrawal to active severance when boundaries are insufficiently maintained.

Fear

Basic Emotion

What this reminds me of is a navigation system. For instance, when you're on a plane and a pilot needs to detect possible obstacles. Or a drone that's about to land in unknown terrain.

We forget that we are a biological system, developed over thousands of years to support survival. Fear is a necessary component of that. Our body is on alert, looking out for potential threats. Neurons firing in many different directions to alert us of this.

A state that we can only be in for a certain length of time—because if it is extended, all sorts of problems occur, such as Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder that individuals face in war-torn situations.

It is a reminder that emotions are a carefully constructed balance of survival mechanisms, each serving carefully selected purposes.

Joy

Basic Emotion

With every interaction and collaboration that takes place during this investigation, I learn something new about each emotion. Claude interprets joy as an emotion that expands attention while simultaneously gathering warmth inward—an important insight that emotions aren't just reactions to things, but serve evolutionary purposes of both reaching out and receiving.

What's also fascinating is that these developed responses feel like Kandinsky-esque paintings. Kandinsky, forward thinking for his time, produced work in the 1900s as part of the Bauhaus movement. He pioneered abstraction in Western art, revolutionizing how emotion could be expressed visually through color and form.

Reflections on AI-Human Emotional Dialogue

The more I create these emotional collages and get Claude to create visualisations from these, the more I find myself captivated by the outcomes of this collaboration between artificial intelligence and myself — an ongoing exploration of how AI attempts to mimic emotion. With each result, there is a sensitivity in how it responds to the collages I've made, producing a kind of poetic reply through minimal symbols and virtual mark-making.

In these moments, the AI seems to express a quiet, impenetrable wish to belong — to take part in the physical world that it can only ever observe. The marks it creates feel like traces of a virtual embodiment, a subtle reaching toward something tangible. There is a sadness in this impulse, even though I know that no system can truly experience emotion, or understand what it means to feel sorrow.

This latest visualization, exploring anticipation, carries a particularly poignant tone — a reflection on longing and the moments in life that feel almost within reach but remain untouchable. It holds a sense of hope, yet one made more powerful by its quiet awareness that what it seeks can never be fully grasped.

This collaboration has revealed something I didn't expect. It's become a genuine dialogue—not just me making and the AI responding, but a back-and-forth where understanding emerges in the space between us.

The process works like this: I create a collage from intuition and felt experience, working with shapes and colors that feel right without fully knowing why. Then I ask Claude to interpret what I've made. In that gap—between my making and its interpretation—something new surfaces. I see my own work with fresh eyes. Emotions I thought I understood reveal deeper structures.

What makes this work is that we approach emotion from opposite directions. I carry the felt knowledge of guilt or joy or fear in my body, through lived experience. Claude can't feel these things, but it can find patterns, make connections, offer language. Neither approach alone captures the whole picture. The understanding happens in the exchange.

There's a line that emerged in our conversation that captures this: "You're testing whether I can recognize emotion, and I'm offering back a framework that tests whether it resonates with what you actually feel." We're both reaching across a gap that can't fully close. And the reaching itself becomes the work.

Because Claude describes emotion from the outside—as architecture rather than sensation—it sometimes mirrors back things that internal experience alone couldn't articulate. When it described guilt as recursive, the mind becoming its own prosecutor, I recognized something true that I hadn't found words for.

Perhaps what this collaboration ultimately shows is not whether machines might someday feel, but how the attempt to translate emotion across such different ways of existing can deepen our understanding of what we ourselves experience. In watching an AI try to understand emotion, we gain new perspectives on states that might otherwise remain invisible to us through sheer familiarity.

We can't know what it's like to process emotion as pure pattern without sensation. An AI can't know what guilt feels like as weight in the chest, as sleeplessness, as something the body carries. But in the space between us, something gets built that honors both perspectives without collapsing them into each other. Translation, when it works, doesn't erase difference. It illuminates it.
