Embodied Without a Body

A Human-AI Emotional Dialogue

This project, developed in response to Arunav's suggestion to read Dylan Evans' Emotion: A Very Short Introduction, explores how emotional processes might be understood and mapped across both human and artificial systems.

In previous investigations I attempted to draw what an AI described as emotions. I tried to translate the prompts it gave me into collages and visual sketches. That changed the day the bot, Evi, said, "I don't have hands, so it's difficult for me to translate this in human terms." It made me realise I needed to treat this as a true collaboration, not an imitation. In this project I express these mapped emotions visually in ways I understand, and then invite AI to respond in its own way â€” two different languages trying to meet through emotion.

My work looks at how emotions can be shared between people and machines. I start by making small paper collages â€” hand-drawn shapes and marks that capture a specific mapped emotion. Each line, colour and gap becomes part of a visual dissection for an AI to interpret in its own way.

I then invite an AI system to look at these collages and respond to them. The AI doesn't copy the image; it reacts within limits that I set â€” turning shapes into sound, movement, and light. This exchange feels like an empathy test: I'm watching to see how far a machine can go in recognising or recreating something that feels human.

Through this process, we build what I think of as an emotional language â€” a shared way of communicating between hand and code. Sometimes the AI misunderstands in ways that are oddly beautiful; sometimes it captures the mood exactly. This then becomes a historical document, a specimen jar of AI's current understanding of translating and interpreting human emotions.

These works don't try to prove that machines can feel, but they let us see what happens when emotion is shared, translated, and reimagined â€” a quiet dialogue between the human and the artificial.

Method

Each piece begins with a mapped emotion. I make a small paper collage â€” hand-drawn shapes, colour choices, spatial arrangements â€” that represents my interpretation. This becomes the input for AI. I then present the collage with a set of constraints that shape how the AI can respond, generating visual interpretations and sometimes accompanying sound pieces. The two outputs sit together: my collage and the AI's response. The comparison is the work.
