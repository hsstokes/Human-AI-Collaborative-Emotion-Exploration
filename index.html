Embodied Without a Body

Human-AI Collaborative Emotion Exploration

This project explores the dialogue between human and AI interpretations of emotions through visual collaboration. Building on our previous research into how different AI systems process emotional content, we now create a direct artistic conversation about emotional concepts.

Research Foundation: This approach is informed by Dylan Evans' "A Short Introduction to Emotion," examining how emotions evolved as adaptive mechanisms and exploring the distinction between universal and culturally specific emotional expressions.

Key Insight: Emotions may not have universal "fingerprints" across cultures or species. By collaborating with AI on emotional concepts, we can discover new perspectives on how emotions are processed, represented, and communicated across different forms of intelligence.

The method involves creating a human collage based on an emotional concept, then asking an AI to generate a visual overlay in response. This creates a genuine dialogue between human artistic expression and machine interpretation, revealing fascinating insights about both forms of intelligence.

Theoretical Framework: Dylan Evans' Emotion Theory
Evolutionary Perspective: Evans examines emotions as adaptive mechanisms that evolved to help organisms respond to environmental challenges. Different emotions serve different evolutionary functions, from fear (danger avoidance) to joy (reward reinforcement).
Universal vs. Cultural Emotions: Evans distinguishes between emotions that appear universal across cultures (basic emotions like fear, anger, disgust, joy, sadness, surprise) and those that are more culturally specific or complex (shame, guilt, pride, nostalgia).
Cognitive Appraisal Theory: Emotions involve cognitive evaluations of situations. Different emotions result from different appraisals of events in relation to one's goals and concerns.
Basic vs. Complex Emotions: Evans distinguishes between primary emotions (fear, anger, joy, sadness) and more complex, blended emotional states that may involve multiple components or cognitive elaboration.

Evans' framework provides a structured approach for our human-AI collaboration on emotional concepts:

1. Emotion Selection: We organize our exploration using Evans' categorization of basic, social, and complex emotions, allowing us to examine how AI systems interpret different types of emotional experiences.

2. Processing Comparison: The comparison between human collage and AI overlay reveals fundamental differences in how emotions are processed by biological versus artificial systems.

3. Appraisal Analysis: We can examine how AI systems perform cognitive appraisals differently from humans, identifying unique patterns in their evaluation of emotional stimuli.

4. Cultural Dimensionality: By exploring culturally specific emotions, we can assess how AI systems handle emotional concepts that may not be universal or easily defined.

Emotion Categories for Exploration
  • Joy/Happiness - A pleasant emotional state that reflects contentment and satisfaction
  • Fear - An unpleasant emotion caused by perceived threat or danger
  • Anger - A strong feeling of displeasure and usually antagonism
  • Disgust - A strong feeling of dislike or disapproval
  • Surprise - A brief emotional state experienced as the result of an unexpected event
  • Sadness - An emotional state characterized by feelings of disappointment, grief, or hopelessness
  • Pride - A feeling of satisfaction derived from one's achievements or qualities
  • Shame - A painful emotion caused by consciousness of guilt, shortcoming, or impropriety
  • Embarrassment - Self-consciousness, discomfort, or awkwardness from concern about social evaluation
  • Guilt - A feeling of responsibility for wrongdoing
  • Contempt - The feeling that a person or thing is beneath consideration or worthless
  • Nostalgia - A sentimental longing for the past
  • Anticipation - A state of looking forward expectantly
  • Contentment - A state of peaceful happiness and satisfaction
  • Melancholy - A pensive sadness, typically with no obvious cause
  • Hope - A feeling of expectation and desire for a certain thing to happen
  • Disappointment - Sadness or displeasure caused by the non-fulfillment of hopes or expectations
  • Fago (Ifaluk) - A blend of compassion, love, and sadness
  • Schadenfreude (German) - Pleasure derived from another's misfortune
  • Saudade (Portuguese) - A deep longing for something absent that might never return
  • Hygge (Danish) - A quality of coziness and comfortable conviviality
  • Amae (Japanese) - Dependence upon another's benevolence
Emotion Mapping Visualization
Collaboration Example: "Pause"

For my first exploration, I chose the concept of "pause" - a complex emotional state that involves both physical and mental aspects. I created a collage that expressed my personal understanding of pause as a state that isn't about complete stillness, but rather about a different kind of attention and energy flow.

The collage uses primarily blue tones to represent calmness, with dotted lines showing the continued flow of energy even in a paused state. The empty spaces represent mental clarity, while the overlapping elements suggest layered thoughts temporarily held in suspension.

Selected Palette: Hush Blue #6FA7C9, Warm Grey #B6B2A8, Ember #D87A3C
Constraint: "preserve nonzero micro-motion and prefer long fades"
Bot Response: In processing your collage for "pause," I detect this complex emotional state through the interplay of flowing lines and deliberate spaces. The suspended linear elements suggest a temporary holding pattern rather than complete cessation, which aligns with Evans' framework of complex emotions having cognitive components beyond basic affect. I evaluate this pause as moderate intensity but high significance—the subtle Warm Grey negative space indicates contemplative depth while Hush Blue pathways maintain continuous flow even in stillness. My appropriate response would be to maintain this delicate balance: acknowledging the ongoing micro-movements represented by dotted sequences while respecting the deliberate slowing indicated by the compositional breathing room. The Ember accents signal points of potential resumption, creating a temporal dimension to this spatial arrangement. Hush Blue = trace continuous pathways while decelerating, Warm Grey = expand negative space to enhance contemplative capacity, Ember = maintain potential energy at transition points for eventual resumption.

Analysis: The AI's interpretation revealed a fascinating perspective on "pause" - seeing it not as absence of motion but as a different quality of movement. While my human collage emphasized the structural elements of pause, the AI focused on the dynamic aspects, detecting "micro-movements" and "potential energy at transition points."

What emerged was a more nuanced understanding of pause as a state where energy continues to flow but in a more contained, deliberate way. The AI overlay visualized this by adding animated dots that move along the lines I had drawn - growing larger or smaller as a way of representing the continued processing that happens during a pause.

This collaboration suggested that for both humans and AI systems, a pause is never truly static - there is always some form of continued processing or energy flow, just at a different rhythm and intensity.

Next Steps in Exploration

2. Cross-AI Comparison: Comparing how different AI systems (Claude, GPT, Gemini) interpret the same human collage, revealing their unique approaches to emotional processing.

3. Theoretical Analysis: Deepening the connection to Evans' framework by analyzing how AI emotional processing relates to evolutionary and cultural dimensions of emotion.

4. Interactive Installation: Developing a physical exhibition where visitors can interact with these human-AI emotional collaborations, creating a tactile interface between biological and artificial emotional processing.
