Embodied Without a Body

A Human-AI Emotional Dialogue

This exploration began after Arunav Das, a PhD researcher I am collaborating with, recommended I read Dylan Evans' Emotion: A Very Short Introduction. He suggested it might be interesting to map emotions against AI — an idea that sparked this project.

In previous investigations I attempted to draw what an AI described as emotions. That changed the day the bot, Evi, said, "I don't have hands, so it's difficult for me to translate this in human terms." It made me realise I needed to treat this as a true collaboration, not an imitation. In this project I express these mapped emotions visually in ways I understand, and then invite AI to respond in its own way — two different languages trying to meet through emotion.

My work looks at how emotions can be shared between people and machines. I start by making small paper collages — hand-drawn shapes and marks that capture a specific mapped emotion. Each is housed in a wooden specimen box with an embedded NFC tag that launches the digital response when scanned.

These works don't try to prove that machines can feel, but they let us see what happens when emotion is shared, translated, and reimagined — a quiet dialogue between the human and the artificial.

Method

Each piece begins with a mapped emotion. I make a small paper collage — hand-drawn shapes, colour choices, spatial arrangements — that represents my interpretation. This becomes the input for AI. I then present the collage with a set of constraints that shape how the AI can respond, generating visual interpretations and accompanying sound pieces. The two outputs sit together: my collage and the AI's response. The comparison is the work.

Reflections on AI-Human Emotional Dialogue

The more I create these emotional collages and get Claude to create visualisations from these, the more I find myself captivated by the outcomes of this collaboration between artificial intelligence and myself — an ongoing exploration of how AI attempts to mimic emotion. With each result, there is a sensitivity in how it responds to the collages I've made, producing a kind of poetic reply through minimal symbols and virtual mark-making.

In these moments, the AI seems to express a quiet, impenetrable wish to belong — to take part in the physical world that it can only ever observe. The marks it creates feel like traces of a virtual embodiment, a subtle reaching toward something tangible. There is a sadness in this impulse, even though I know that no system can truly experience emotion, or understand what it means to feel sorrow.

This collaboration has revealed something I didn't expect — a genuine dialogue, not just me making and AI responding, but an exchange where understanding emerges in the space between us.

We approach emotion from opposite directions. I carry the felt knowledge of guilt, joy, or fear in my body, through lived experience. Claude cannot feel these things, but it can find patterns, make connections, offer language. Neither approach alone captures the whole picture. The understanding happens in the exchange.

What this collaboration ultimately shows is not whether machines might someday feel, but how the attempt to translate emotion across such different ways of existing can deepen our understanding of what we ourselves experience. Translation, when it works, doesn't erase difference. It illuminates it.
